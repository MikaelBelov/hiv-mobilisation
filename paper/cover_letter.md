Dear Editors,

Russia's war in Ukraine is generating HIV infections thousands of kilometres from the front line. We submit the first causal evidence for this claim: "Military mobilisation and HIV transmission in Russia: a difference-in-differences study."

Regions that sent more men to war experienced 17% more HIV among pregnant women than expected. The effect is not driven by drug use, not by bereavement, and not by a general deterioration of healthcare — it operates through one channel: soldiers returning to their sexual partners. Internal Russian military medical data, published and then removed from the Ministry of Defence website, document a 40-fold increase in HIV among armed forces personnel. Our civilian data show where that virus goes when soldiers come home.

We believe The Lancet Public Health is the right home for this work for three reasons.

First, timing. Your sister journal published an editorial in October 2025 describing Russia as "spiralling into an HIV crisis," with data withheld from WHO and antiretroviral coverage at 50%. Our paper provides a specific, quantified mechanism behind that crisis: the war itself is generating new infections, and it is doing so in the poorest regions that bear the weakest HIV response infrastructure.

Second, the policy gap we identify is concrete and actionable. The US military has required deployment-linked HIV testing since the late 1990s. Russia has no equivalent — and internal military medical data document a 40-fold increase in HIV among armed forces personnel, with screening requirements bypassed during recruitment. We recommend extending pre- and post-deployment HIV testing to any country conducting large-scale mobilisation, and broadening UNAIDS military HIV frameworks from peacekeeping operations to active conflict.

Third, this is an equity story. The regions that supply the most soldiers — Buryatia, Tuva, Dagestan, Mari El — are among Russia's poorest and most marginalised. They bear the heaviest military losses and now, as we show, the heaviest HIV burden. The causal chain runs from poverty to recruitment to infection and back to the same communities. The women who become infected are partners of returning soldiers — indirect casualties of a war they did not choose to fight.
